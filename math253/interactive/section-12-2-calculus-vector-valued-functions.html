Section 12.2: Calculus and Vector-Valued Functions

Exploring derivatives, integrals, and tangent vectors in space

Section Overview

In this section, we delve into the calculus of vector-valued functions. We extend the concepts of limits, derivatives, and integrals from real-valued functions to vector-valued functions, enabling us to analyze motion in space with the tools of calculus.

Key Concepts in This Section

  • Limits of Vector-Valued Functions: The limit of a vector-valued function $\mathbf{r}(t)$ as $t$ approaches a value is defined using the norm (magnitude) of the difference between vectors.
  • Continuity: A vector-valued function $\mathbf{r}(t)$ is continuous at a point if the limit equals the function's value at that point.
  • Derivatives: The derivative $\mathbf{r}'(t)$ measures the instantaneous rate of change and provides a vector tangent to the curve.
  • Tangent Vectors and Lines: The tangent vector at a point is parallel to $\mathbf{r}'(t)$, used to find tangent line equations.
  • Integration: Integrals provide displacement, and arc length can be found by integrating the magnitude of the derivative.
  • Properties of Derivatives: Linearity, product rules, and chain rules extend to vector-valued functions.

Worked Example: Finding the Derivative

Example: Derivative of $\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle t^2, \sin(t), e^t \rangle$

Key Concept: By Definition 12.2.8, we differentiate each component separately.

Solution:

  • First component: $\frac{d}{dt}(t^2) = 2t$
  • Second component: $\frac{d}{dt}(\sin(t)) = \cos(t)$
  • Third component: $\frac{d}{dt}(e^t) = e^t$

Answer: $\mathbf{r}'(t) = \langle 2t, \cos(t), e^t \rangle$

Why this works: Theorem 12.2.9 guarantees that a vector function is differentiable if each component is differentiable. For more complex operations, see Theorem 12.2.20 on derivative properties.
