Vector Form of a Line

A line in 3D space can be expressed using a position vector $\vec{r_0}$ to a point on the line and a direction vector $\vec{v}$:

$$\vec{r}(t) = \vec{r_0} + t\vec{v}$$

Where $t$ is a parameter that ranges over all real numbers, giving every point on the line.

Parametric Equations

The vector form can be expanded into three parametric equations for the coordinates:

$$x = x_0 + at$$ $$y = y_0 + bt$$ $$z = z_0 + ct$$

Where $(x_0, y_0, z_0)$ is a point on the line and $\langle a, b, c \rangle$ is the direction vector.

📖 Example 11.5.6: Finding Line Through Two Points

Symmetric Form

When none of the direction vector components are zero, we can eliminate the parameter:

$$\frac{x - x_0}{a} = \frac{y - y_0}{b} = \frac{z - z_0}{c}$$

This symmetric form directly shows the relationship between coordinates without the parameter $t$.

Finding Line Equations

To find a line equation, you need:

  • One point on the line
  • A direction vector (or two points to find it)
  • Or two planes that intersect to form the line

The direction vector can be found from two points: $\vec{v} = P_2 - P_1$

📐 Essential Formulas

Distance from Point to Line

$$d = \frac{|\vec{PQ} \times \vec{v}|}{|\vec{v}|}$$

Where $P$ is the external point, $Q$ is any point on the line, and $\vec{v}$ is the direction vector.

📖 Key Idea 11.5.14: Distances to Lines

Distance between Skew Lines

$$d = \frac{|(\vec{P_1} - \vec{P_2}) \cdot (\vec{v_1} \times \vec{v_2})|}{|\vec{v_1} \times \vec{v_2}|}$$

For non-parallel, non-intersecting lines with direction vectors $\vec{v_1}$ and $\vec{v_2}$.

📖 Example 11.5.16: Distance Between Lines

Angle between Lines

$$\cos \theta = \frac{|\vec{v_1} \cdot \vec{v_2}|}{|\vec{v_1}||\vec{v_2}|}$$

The acute angle between two lines with direction vectors $\vec{v_1}$ and $\vec{v_2}$.

🌍 Real-World Applications

✈️ Flight Path Navigation Systems

Commercial aviation relies heavily on 3D line equations for flight path planning, air traffic control, and automated navigation systems. Aircraft trajectories are modeled as parametric lines where the direction vector represents velocity and the initial point is the departure location.

Real Project: FAA's Automatic Dependent Surveillance-Broadcast (ADS-B) system uses precise 3D positioning and trajectory calculations to track aircraft in real-time, preventing collisions and optimizing flight paths.

🎮 Computer Graphics & Ray Tracing

Modern 3D rendering engines use line equations extensively in ray tracing algorithms. Each light ray is modeled as a parametric line, and intersection calculations with 3D objects determine realistic lighting, shadows, and reflections in computer-generated imagery.

Real Project: NVIDIA's RTX Ray Tracing technology implements real-time ray tracing in video games and professional rendering, using millions of line-object intersection calculations per second to create photorealistic graphics.

🏗️ Structural Engineering & Architecture

Engineers use 3D line equations to model support beams, cables, and structural elements in complex buildings and bridges. Distance calculations between skew lines help determine clearances, stress distributions, and optimal placement of structural components.

Real Project: Bentley STAAD structural analysis software uses advanced 3D line mathematics to simulate and analyze the behavior of complex structures like skyscrapers, bridges, and stadiums under various load conditions.
